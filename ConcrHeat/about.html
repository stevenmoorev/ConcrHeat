Project Statement

The project “ConcrHeat” is an art installation for the class CART 411 that focuses on the “Urban Heat Island” (UHI) effect in large cities such as Montreal. In particular, the effect was seen during the July 2018 heatwave where temperatures reached up to the mid-40’s with little to no relief from the weather. The primarily digital installation simulates the lingering effects and the interception of humans in relation to the UHI effect. The project is an abstract reconstruction of Montreal placed on a table, where users hover their hand over or touch. The feedback creates a generative abstract art depending on the section of Montreal that spreads towards other parts of the abstract city. Certain sections of the city create art that spreads that is representational of the pervasive heat in Montreal. However, to bring an element of contrast with the positive effect of highly nature centric areas of the city, there is an opposing effect when the user hovers/touches over these specific areas. To add a sensation of the UHI effect, there will be tactile feedback of heat from the installation (within a safe range) along with an ambient soundscape that will be altered according to the feedback of the abstract piece.

Project Description

“ConcrHeat” (ˈkänˌkrēt,ˌkänˈkrēt) is an interactive art piece made by Julie Babineau, Rochelle Panganiban, Maedeh Sharifi, Gerald Alvarez and Steven Moore-Vountas as a part of their senior-year Computation Arts project at Concordia University. The project puts a 100 square kilometers of central Montreal into focus in a one square meter area carved into wood, painted with green-to-white thermochromic ink, and laced with heat wire. ConcrHeat is an interactive audio-visual and sensory art piece that utilizes Max application and a projector, borrowed from the Centre for Digital Arts, for visual and auditory outputs and an Arduino Mega microprocessor for sensory functionality, all controlled by a computer. The projection will hitherto change with the interaction, depending on which area is hovered over. A generative fractal art will be projected on the subjects’ hands as they cast shade on the map from the projector’s rays, and finally, sounds will help enhance the experience.

The topographical map of Montreal from Hampstead to Villeray to the North, and from Lasalle to the Islands to the South, is composed of twelve superposed sheets of Baltic Birch Plywood, cut with a CNC wood router at the Fabrication Lab, and glued together. It is raised off the ground to bring spectators’ hands closer, and to hide the speaker and computer under the table, made at the Concordia Woodshop with as much reused wood as possible from CUCCR or other sources. The sheets represent height to scale. The maquette is divided into regions, or neighbourhoods, with a set of light sensors, that will be programmed at the Sensor Lab. An average of light received by the nearby sensors send information to the laptop borrowed from the CDA to trigger the on/off switch for the heat conductive wires, representing the main transportation arteries used by cars, trucks and trains in that region. It also locates where the generative art shall be projected over the region to display our heat or cooling imprint on the environment.

Furthermore, a Max application creates projected images of algorithmically-generated trees on the hands. The user creates shade over the maquette, mimicking the phenomenon trees produce in areas such as parks that naturally cool the area. In absence of user interaction, a satellite image of our chosen area will be projected on the maquette and ambient cityscape sounds will play out of a speaker hidden under the table. If a user interacts with ConcrHeat, a subtle nature ambient sound is played. If multiple users interact with it, the nature sound subdues the city ambient sound. If the audience ventures further closer to the model and touch it, they will feel the effect of the heat wires permeating into the ply, but also leave a slightly lingering imprint due to the thermochromic paint on the maquette.
